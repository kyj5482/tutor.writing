# Grade 8 Examples — *The Giver* by Lois Lowry

All examples below imagine an 8th grader who read **chapters 1–4** of *The Giver*:
Jonas lives in a perfectly ordered community where everything — jobs, families, even
feelings — is controlled. He is nervous about the upcoming Ceremony of Twelve, where
his Assignment (lifelong job) will be announced. His father, a Nurturer, has brought
home a struggling newchild named Gabriel. Small cracks are showing: an apple once
seemed to "change" in midair, and people who break rules are "released."

Examples are written at **Tier 2–3**. They're strong but human — your writing should
sound like YOU, not like these.

---

## 01 — Summary

In the first four chapters of *The Giver*, Lois Lowry introduces Jonas's community,
which looks peaceful but feels slightly wrong. Jonas is searching for the right word
for what he feels about the December Ceremony, and settles on "apprehensive" rather
than "frightened." At evening telling-of-feelings, his father admits he is worried
about a newchild named Gabriel who may be released, and his sister Lily complains
about a visiting child who broke the rules. Later, Jonas remembers the time an apple
he was tossing with Asher seemed to *change* in midair — something he could never
explain. Finally, Jonas does volunteer hours bathing an old woman named Larissa, who
cheerfully describes the "release" of an elderly man as a celebration. What matters
about these chapters is the gap between how calm everyone sounds and how many
disturbing things they're calmly describing.

## 02 — Prediction

I have two predictions. What I *hope* happens is that Jonas gets assigned to work
with his father so he can protect Gabriel, because the book keeps emphasizing how
fragile Gabriel is. What the author is *probably* setting up is something stranger:
Jonas will get an unusual Assignment connected to the apple incident. My evidence is
that the apple "had changed, in mid-air" — and the narrator brings it up out of
nowhere, which is the kind of detail authors only plant when it will pay off. Lowry's
pattern so far is to make normal scenes feel slightly off, so I don't think she's
writing toward a comfortable ending. The strange prediction fits the book better
than the happy one.

## 03 — Character Diary

They would never let me keep a diary like this. Honesty is required out loud, at the
evening meal, where everyone can hear it and smooth it over. This is different. This
is mine.

Tonight Father said Gabriel might be released, and he said it in the same voice he
uses to describe his lunch. I nodded the way you're supposed to nod. But something
in my chest went tight, and I don't have a word for it — and I'm someone who is
careful with words. Lily would laugh at me. Asher would make it a joke. So I'll
write the secret here instead: when Father said "released," for half a second I
didn't believe his calmness. I don't even know what that means, to not believe
calmness. The Ceremony is in December. I keep telling myself apprehensive is the
precise word. It's getting less precise every day.
— Jonas

## 04 — Opinion / Response

The community in *The Giver* trades too much freedom for safety, and the evening
"telling of feelings" proves it. When Jonas's family shares emotions at dinner, every
feeling gets named, discussed, and dissolved — Lily's anger at the visiting child is
talked away in minutes. This shows that the ritual isn't really about understanding
feelings; it's about disposing of them before they grow into anything inconvenient.
A second piece of evidence is Larissa describing a release as if it were a graduation
party. The community has made even loss feel tidy, which means the people can't
actually be choosing their reactions at all. Some people might say the community is
simply kind — nobody starves, nobody is bullied, everyone has a role. But kindness
you cannot refuse is just control with better manners. The scariest thing in these
chapters isn't a villain; it's that there is no villain to point at.

## 05 — Letter to a Character

Dear Jonas,
When you corrected yourself from "frightened" to "apprehensive," I actually stopped
reading for a second. You said precision of language matters — but have you noticed
that your community uses precise words to make big things sound small? "Released"
is a precise word too. Precisely empty. Here are my two questions: Why did no one
else seem to notice the apple change, and why didn't you push harder for an answer?
I'm writing as a worried friend, not a critic — you notice things other people
don't, and I don't think that's an accident. Be careful at the Ceremony. I know
something you don't: the adults' calm is hiding something, and you're getting close
to it.
Your friend, Dana

P.S. If anyone asks, this letter never existed. You'd be assigned extra apologies
for reading it.

## 06 — Rewrite the Scene (What If…?)

In the book, when the apple changes in midair, Jonas keeps it quiet and the moment
passes. But what if Asher had seen it too?

The apple left Jonas's hand, hung at the top of its arc, and — flickered. Not
moved. Flickered, the way a fish flashes its side at the sun.

Asher didn't catch it. The apple hit the ground and rolled against his foot, and
he was staring at it with his mouth open.

"You saw," Jonas said quietly.

"I didn't see anything." Asher said it too fast, the way he recited his morning
apologies. He picked up the apple, turned it over twice, and set it down on the
bench as if it were hot. "We should go. It's nearly time for —"

"Asher."

His friend looked up, and for once there was no joke arriving in his face. "My
instructor told me," Asher whispered, "that some words we're not supposed to use
aren't rude, Jonas. They're *dangerous*. I think that apple is one of them."

Neither of them noticed the Speaker's camera above the bench, rotating slowly
toward the sound of their voices.

## 07 — Golden Line

**My golden line:** "We really have to protect people from wrong choices."
(chapter 4-ish, Larissa's bathing scene area — the community's logic)

What's happening: Jonas and the adults around him constantly justify the rules, and
this line distills the entire community into nine words. The craft move I notice is
the word "protect" — Lowry could have written "stop" or "prevent," but "protect"
is the word a loving parent uses, which is exactly what makes it chilling. The
sentence sounds warm and is actually a cage. It connects to the bigger pattern of
the book so far: every disturbing thing is wrapped in comfortable language —
"release," "Nurturer," "Stirrings." My imitation sentence in the same style: "We
really have to protect people from their own questions."

## 08 — Connection

In the book, the community assigns every twelve-year-old their lifelong job at a
single ceremony, and everyone applauds whatever the Elders decide. This connects to
a bigger idea: the trade-off between security and choice. In my own life, the
closest thing is course selection — this year I had to choose between band and a
coding elective, and I spent two weeks miserable about it. The difference is the
point: my misery was *mine*. Jonas will never get to be miserable over a choice,
and the book is starting to suggest that's a loss, not a gift. My anchor quote is
Jonas deciding "apprehensive" is more precise than "frightened" — he's allowed to
fine-tune his words but not his life. The question the book is really asking is:
if someone removed every chance for you to choose wrong, would you still be you?
My honest answer today is no — I'd be a very well-behaved photograph of me.

## 09 — Quick Write 3-2-1 (for a short-reading day)

**3 things that happened:**
1. Jonas rejected "frightened" and chose "apprehensive" to describe his feeling
   about the December Ceremony.
2. Father revealed he might bring the struggling newchild Gabriel home, since
   Gabriel is at risk of being released.
3. Jonas did volunteer hours at the House of the Old, where Larissa described a
   release celebration.

**2 words:** "transgression" — from context (the apologies and rules), I'd guess it
means breaking a rule. "Chastisement" — I think it means a scolding or punishment,
because it follows misbehavior.

**1 question:** What actually happens during a release? My guess: nobody in the
community knows the real answer either — and the author is hiding it on purpose,
because Larissa describes the ceremony but admits she doesn't know where the
released man went.

**So what?** The detail that mattered most today was Larissa not knowing where
released people go. A community obsessed with precise language has no precise
answer for its most important word. That gap can't be an accident, because Lowry
has been exact about everything else.

## 10 — Book Review *(example written as if the whole book was finished)*

What would you give up for a world with no war, no pain, and no fear? *The Giver*
by Lois Lowry answers: more than you think. Twelve-year-old Jonas lives in a
community where every choice is made for you — until he receives an Assignment
that forces him to see what his perfect world threw away. That's all I can say
without spoiling it.

What the book does brilliantly is its slow reveal: Lowry writes the first chapters
in the community's own calm, soothing voice, so you feel comfortable right up until
you realize what you've been nodding along to. The first-person-adjacent narration
(we only know what Jonas knows) makes every discovery land at full force. The one
weakness is the ending, which is so open that it feels less like a choice and more
like a shrug — I wanted one more page.

It loses half a star because of that ending, but it earns the other 4.5 because no
other book made me reread my own normal life like a suspicious document. 4.5/5.
If you liked *The Hunger Games* but wished it was quieter and creepier, read this.
